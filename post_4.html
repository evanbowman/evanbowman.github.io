Pyrrole Red

A few weeks ago, I started using Winsor and Newton's Bright Red (PR254), and based on my experience so far, I do not think that I will ever use Cadmium paints again. A lot of painters are moving away from Cadmium-based pigments due to concerns over toxicity—concerns that seem a little overblown to me. My own disinclination toward cadmium paints stems from other properties of the pigments; my impression is that cadmiums are just difficult to mix with. I dropped the yellow cadmiums from my palette shortly after I started painting, because I feel that bright yellows impart an immature and superficial quality on artwork. I also tended to avoid bright reds for the same reason, but still kept Cadmium-Red around for occasional use—the medium variant is quite nice.

Pyrrole reds are just as saturated, in the masstone, as cadmiums, but lean more toward pure primary red. And Pyrrole's transparency lends itself better to producing clean grays. I find myself using Pyrrole sometimes for bright accents in still-life, and more frequently for figure painting. I sketched a few copies of Velazquez portraits last month, and enjoyed using a palette containing Pyrrole (PR254):

  • Flake White Hue (PW4+PW6)
  • Yellow Ocher (PY42)
  • Transparent Gold Ocher (PY42+PY43)
  • Burnt Umber (PBr7)
  • Burnt Sienna (PBr7)
  • Bright Red (PR254)
  • Viridian (PG18)
  • Ivory Black (PBK7)
